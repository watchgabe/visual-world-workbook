Part 1 — Foundation · Chapter 1

The strategy behind the content

Content strategy isn't about what to post and when. It's about understanding why you're creating, who it's for, and what you want them to do after they consume it. Without that clarity, you're creating for the sake of creating — and that's how you burn out.

The trust-first framework

Optimizing for virality is not the same as optimizing for customers. When you optimize for virality, you make wide, broad content designed to appeal to everyone — and it converts no one. When you optimize for trust, you make specific, deep content designed to solve real problems for a specific group. Fewer views. But the people who watch will believe you can help them. And belief is what drives purchases.

A thousand raving fans is much better than a hundred thousand followers who don't know who you actually are — or care.

The content formula

Painful Problem + Unique Solution × Contextual Credibility + Proven Wrapper = Content That Gets Customers

  • Painful Problem: What specific problem does this content solve?
  • Unique Solution: How do you solve it differently than everyone else?
  • Contextual Credibility: Why should they believe you on this specific topic — not general credentials, direct experience with this problem.
  • Proven Wrapper: How are you packaging it to get attention? (Covered in Part 3.)

Part 1 — Foundation · Chapters 2 & 3

Your sustainable foundation

73% of people who start creating content quit within their first year — not because they ran out of ideas, but because they built a system that wasn't sustainable. Build around your floor, not your ceiling.

Why most creators quit

The goal is simple: build a system so frictionless it runs on your worst week — not your best. Most creators design for motivation spikes and imaginary future capacity. The fix is to strip everything back and answer your capacity questions without optimism. Don't count weeks where things slow down. Don't count imaginary future hires. Don't count motivation that won't last. Your system must work when you're tired, busy, and uninspired. Simplify until it feels inevitable — then add from there.

Your focal point

At any given moment, your brand should have one clear focal point. One platform gets your best thinking, your best energy, and your best content. Everything else is secondary or repurposed. Trying to dominate every platform at once is how you spread thin, create diluted content, and burn out before you ever build momentum.

  • Solo creator: One primary platform (best thinking + full effort). One secondary platform (repurposed content only).
  • With a team: Two to three primary platforms — but only one in full focus at a time.

Your focal point only points one direction at a time. Pick your platform based on what medium feels most natural to you and where your ideal customer actually spends time. Then go all in.

Part 2 — Visual Identity

Tips for Cinematic Content

Our goal is to make your everyday look premium. People judge your content before they even watch it. If it looks cheap, generic, or inconsistent, they'll assume your offer is too. You don't need expensive gear — you need intentional decisions. Here's exactly what those decisions are.

Camera settings that matter

  • Frame rate: 24fps for the cinematic "movie look." 60fps or 120fps only when you plan to slow down footage for B-roll.
  • Shutter speed: Always double your frame rate. 24fps = 1/50. 60fps = 1/120. This keeps motion blur natural and cinematic.
  • Aperture: f1.8–f2.8 for talking head content — gives you background blur and cinematic depth. Lower f-stop = more blur.

Lighting: setting the emotion

Lighting sets the emotion before the viewer even understands the story. Always film facing your light source — window in front of you, not behind. For studio setups: key light at 45 degrees from your face, fill light on the opposite side to soften shadows, optional hair/back light to separate you from the background.

  • Warm golden natural light — comfort, trust, approachability
  • Bright even studio light — clarity, professionalism, focus
  • High contrast dramatic light — energy, urgency, power
  • Soft diffused light — thoughtfulness, depth, calm

Composition: the foundation of premium visuals

Rule of thirds: Divide your frame into nine sections. Place your subject on one of the four intersection points — not dead center. Leave breathing room in the direction you're facing. This immediately makes a shot feel more cinematic.

Depth — the biggest difference between basic and cinematic: Always think in three planes. Foreground (closest to camera, slightly out of focus), middle ground (your subject — sharp), background (the environment, slightly out of focus). Place an object slightly in front of the lens. This creates depth and makes your content feel expensive without expensive gear.

Leading lines: Stairs, railings, building edges, pathways — position yourself so these lines lead the eye through the frame toward you.

Color grading: for emotion, not trends

Color is emotion. Before someone listens to what you're saying, they feel your content. Your grade should reinforce your visual world — not follow what's trending. A consistent grade that's slightly off is better than a perfect grade that changes every video.

  • Warm tones (oranges, yellows, warm whites) — energy, success, approachability
  • Cool tones (blues, grays, cool whites) — authority, clarity, professionalism
  • Desaturated / film-like (muted colors) — raw, honest, authentic
  • High contrast (deep blacks, bright highlights) — cinematic, premium, dramatic
Part 2 — Visual Identity · Chapter 8 — Shots, Angles & B-Roll

Cinematic shots, angles, and B-roll

Every shot you choose communicates something before you say a word. A close-up creates intimacy. A wide shot creates context. A low angle creates power. Understanding the full vocabulary of shots gives you the ability to say exactly what you mean visually.

Shots & Angles

Every shot you choose communicates something before you say a word. Height is power. Distance is intimacy. Use these deliberately — never accidentally.

1. Wide
Full body visible, environment prominent. Establishes location and sense of place. Use for intros, lifestyle, and showing your world.
2. Medium
Waist to head. The standard creator shot — professional, conversational, and present. Your go-to for talking head content.
3. Tight / Close-Up
Face or detail only. Creates intimacy and intensity. Use to drive a point home or capture B-roll details — hands, product, process.
4. Top-Down
Camera directly overhead, looking straight down. Unique and shareable. The go-to for workspace B-roll, flat lays, and process reveals.
5. Low Angle
Camera below eye level, looking up. Makes the subject feel powerful, dominant, larger than life. Use intentionally for authority and cinematic B-roll.
6. High Angle
Camera above eye level, angled down at the subject. Shows both person and environment together. Creates context, scale, and a sense of the subject within their world. Use for workspace reveals, lifestyle moments, and cinematic B-roll.
7. Drone / Aerial
Camera way above — drone or elevated wide perspective. Reveals scale, environment, and geography. Instantly cinematic. Use for location establishing shots, travel content, and any moment that needs to feel massive.
8. Ground Level
Camera at or near the floor, shooting up and across. More extreme than a low angle — the viewer is in the environment, not above it. Creates raw intensity and a gritty, documentary feel.
9. POV (Point of View)
First-person perspective. The camera shows what the subject sees — immersive, intimate, and engaging. Pulls the viewer into the experience. Use for walks, routines, processes, and moments you want the audience to feel they're living.
10. Reverse POV
Camera faces back at the subject from the perspective of what they're looking at. Instead of showing the view, you see the person experiencing it. Creates a powerful sense of reaction, emotion, and presence.

Creative framing techniques

Creative framing turns an ordinary location into a cinematic one. Instead of placing your subject in an open frame, use the environment itself to frame them.

  • Shoot through objects: Place something between the camera and subject — a plant, glass, candle, furniture. Keep it slightly out of focus in the foreground. This creates depth and makes the shot feel expensive immediately.
  • Frame within a frame: Use doorframes, windows, archways, or open books to create a natural frame around your subject. Guides the viewer's eye exactly where you want it.
  • Leading lines: Stairs, hallways, bookshelves, table edges — position yourself so lines in the environment lead toward you and draw the eye through the frame.
  • Shoot through foliage: Plants, leaves, or branches slightly out of focus in the foreground. This single technique makes your content look 10x more cinematic instantly.
  • Reflections: Mirrors, windows, puddles, glass surfaces. Reflections add a second layer to the composition and create unexpected, visually interesting frames.
  • Negative space: Intentionally leave large areas of empty space. Counterintuitive but powerful — creates breathing room, draws attention to your subject, communicates confidence in your composition.

Camera movement

Static shots are safe. Movement creates energy, emotion, and cinematic feel. Every move should have a purpose.

  • Push in (dolly in): Camera moves toward subject. Creates intensity and emphasis. Use on key moments — a big statement, emotional beat, or reveal.
  • Pull out (dolly out): Camera moves away. Creates isolation or scale. Powerful as an ending shot.
  • Pan: Camera rotates left or right on fixed axis. Use to reveal something new in the frame or follow a moving subject.
  • Tilt: Camera rotates up or down. Use for reveals — tilt up to reveal a standing subject, tilt down to reveal a detail.
  • Tracking shot: Camera moves alongside a moving subject. Creates energy and dynamism. Great for walking shots and lifestyle B-roll.
  • Handheld: Camera held without stabilization. Creates a raw, documentary, in-the-moment feel. Use sparingly and intentionally.

B-roll: the most underused tool in content

B-roll is any footage that isn't your main talking head — the visual layer that runs over your voiceover and story moments. Most creators skip B-roll entirely. This is one of the biggest production mistakes you can make, because B-roll is what makes the difference between content that feels like a recording and content that feels like a production.

B-roll does three things simultaneously: it gives your viewer's eyes something new (holds attention), it visually reinforces the story you're telling with your voice, and it makes editing easier because you can cut between A-roll and B-roll to trim dead air without the viewer noticing.

Types of B-roll to capture:

  • Process B-roll: Hands typing, writing, reviewing work on screen. Shows you working — builds credibility through action.
  • Detail / macro shots: Close-ups of objects — coffee cup, book spine, camera lens, keyboard keys. These detail shots give editors visual variety and make content feel textured.
  • Environment B-roll: Wide and medium shots of your workspace or a location you're referencing. Establishes context and visual world.
  • Reaction B-roll: Candid shots of you thinking, reviewing, looking at a screen. Feels authentic and documentary-style.
  • Product / gear B-roll: Slow, deliberate shots of tools you use. At 60fps slowed to 50%, even simple gear shots look cinematic.
  • Lifestyle B-roll: Coffee being made, walking through a space, opening a notebook. These "slice of life" shots humanize you and make content feel personal.
1
Film A-roll first — get your talking head done. Then go back and capture B-roll to cover key moments in your script.
2
Shoot B-roll at 60fps — slowed to 50% in post, even simple shots look cinematic. Easiest production value upgrade.
3
Hold every B-roll shot for 10+ seconds — you'll use 3–5 seconds in editing but you need options on both ends.
4
Capture every subject from 3 angles — wide, close, and overhead. Gives you editing options and keeps visual pace moving.
5
Build a B-roll library — batch capture on filming day and save. Over time you'll have a library to pull from for any piece of content.

Part 3 — Content Creation · Chapters 9–13

Storytelling

You have 2–3 seconds on short-form, 15–20 on long-form. If you don't capture attention in that window, you've lost them forever — and they'll never see the valuable part.

The 4C's intro (long-form)

  • Call Out: Who is this for? Make it explicit. "If you're a creator who's been posting for months without results, this is for you."
  • Credibility: Why should they listen to you on this specific topic? Contextual credibility — not general credentials.
  • Compass: Give them the roadmap. "In this video I'm going to show you the three things making your content look amateur and exactly how to fix them."
  • Core Learning: Give a valuable insight in the first 60 seconds. If they learn something immediately, they'll assume the rest is just as valuable.

The 3-Step Hook Formula

01
Context Lean
Establish the topic. Get them leaning in. Just enough context to understand why they should care.
02
Scroll Stop
Hit them with a contrasting word — But. However. Yet. The pattern interrupt. A sudden gear shift that makes the brain pay attention.
03
Contrarian Snapback
Go the opposite direction. Challenge their assumption. Create a curiosity gap. Make them need to know what's next.

"Most creators spend hours on content that gets zero engagement... BUT it's not because their content is bad. It's because they're solving the wrong problem."

Visual hooks are 100× more powerful than spoken hooks alone. Motion + text on screen + spoken word = three simultaneous pattern interrupts. Rule: 3–5 words max on screen, bold sans-serif, high contrast, first 2 seconds.

The Five-Part Story Framework

Every great piece of content follows a structure. The hook (using your 3-step formula) grabs attention. The problem sets the stakes. The journey is the messy middle most creators skip. The lesson is your expertise made actionable. The CTA tells them what's next. Build one complete story below.

Part 3 — Content Formats

Formats that stop the scroll

Different formats serve different purposes. Short-form builds awareness. Carousels build authority. Long-form builds trust and drives conversions. Use all three — but understand what each one is for.

Short-form content (reels, TikTok, shorts)

Purpose: awareness, trust-building, top of funnel. Length: 15–90 seconds. Goal: stop the scroll, deliver one clear insight, drive to next step. One idea per video — never try to cover everything.

  • Quick Insight: One valuable idea in 30–60 seconds. No fluff. Pure value.
  • Contrarian Take: Challenge a common belief. Creates engagement and positions you as a thinker.
  • Before/After: Show a transformation. Visual proof of your expertise.
  • Process Reveal: Behind-the-scenes of how you do something. Builds trust through transparency.
  • Story Clip: A self-contained moment from a longer story. Creates curiosity for more.

Carousels: the 10-slide structure

  • Slide 1 — Hook Slide: Your thumbnail. Bold headline, clear value proposition, visual that creates curiosity. If slide 1 doesn't stop the scroll, no one sees slide 2.
  • Slide 2 — Promise Slide: Tell them exactly what they'll get. "In this carousel you'll learn X, Y, and Z." Sets expectations and gives them a reason to keep swiping.
  • Slides 3–8 — Value Slides: One idea per slide. Clean, simple, scannable. Bold text for main point, supporting text for context. Don't crowd.
  • Slide 9 — Summary Slide: Recap key points. This is for people who swiped to the end first — and there are a lot of them.
  • Slide 10 — CTA Slide: Tell them what to do next. One CTA only — save, share, follow, or link in bio.

Carousel rules: consistent color palette across all slides, consistent typography, breathing room on every slide, one idea per slide, strong contrast.

Long-form content (YouTube, podcasts)

Purpose: trust-building, authority, conversion. Long-form is where conversion happens — the more time someone spends with you, the more likely they are to buy. Open with your strongest insight. Use B-roll to break up talking head every 60–90 seconds. Vary shot setups (close-up, medium, wide). Music under voiceover sections only.

  • Deep Dive: One topic explored thoroughly. Your positioning video.
  • Framework Video: Teach a complete system. Positions you as someone with a proven method.
  • Case Study: Before, process, after. Pure credibility.
  • Contrarian Argument: Take a position that goes against industry norms.
  • Story Video: Cinematic, narrative-driven. Builds emotional connection.

Part 4 — Content System

The system that keeps you showing up

The waterfall turns one pillar piece into 20 posts across every platform. The 70/20/10 keeps your content mix sharp. The batching system removes the friction that makes most creators quit.

The content waterfall

One pillar piece — typically your long-form video script or newsletter — sits at the top of your waterfall. Everything else flows from it. You don't create 20 pieces of content. You create one great piece and let the system multiply it.

  • 5 short-form tweets — dissect the pillar into punchy standalone ideas
  • 3 short-form video scripts — ~95 words each, cross-posted across Instagram, YouTube Shorts, TikTok, and X (12 posts total)
  • 1 Instagram Carousel — repackage the core framework visually
  • 1 LinkedIn long-form post — adapt for a professional audience
  • 1 Newsletter — expand and send 4–6 weeks after the original

1 pillar piece → 20 posts. Same thinking. Maximum leverage.

The 70/20/10 framework

70%
Proven Content
Formats and topics you know work. Your reliable output — the content your audience expects and engages with consistently. This is your foundation.
20%
Iterations
Same content, different hook, setting, or delivery. Take what's already working and test a new angle. Low risk, high learning.
10%
Big Swings
New formats, new topics, experiments. This is where most creators fail — they get comfortable and stop experimenting. Then their audience gets bored. The 10% keeps you sharp.

The batching system

Protect your maker time. The moment a calendar notification interrupts a creative session, the session is over. Film in the same outfit to batch 2–3 weeks of content at once. Run your week on this system and content stops feeling like a grind.

01
Monday — Research & Ideation
Find your topic, research angles, write rough outlines. This is pure thinking time. No camera, no filming — just building the foundation everything else runs on.
02
Tuesday — Writing & Hooks
Write your script or talking points. Draft 3 hook versions using the 3-step formula. Lock your structure before you touch the camera.
03
Wednesday — Filming
All A-roll in one session. Change outfits to batch 2–3 weeks at once. Capture B-roll after you finish talking head. Treat it like a shoot, not a recording.
04
Thursday — Editing & Post
Cut, color grade, add music, create thumbnails. Review for pacing — kill every moment that loses energy. Export everything in the same session. Or send to your editor if you have one.
05
Friday — Publishing & Engagement
Schedule or publish. Reply to every comment in the first hour — the algorithm rewards it and so does your audience. Track what lands, kill what doesn't.

Maker time is sacred. Deep work, no meetings, no notifications. Your best creative output happens in uninterrupted blocks — protect them like your income depends on it. It does.

Part 5 — Trust & Conversion · Chapters 17–19

How to turn content into customers

Content doesn't sell. Trust sells. Your content is the mechanism for building trust at scale. Every piece either deposits into or withdraws from your trust account with your audience.

The four trust pillars

1. Authority: You know what you're talking about. Build it through frameworks, case studies, and specific results — contextual credibility, not general credentials.

2. Relatability: You've been where they are. Share your origin story, failures, and the messy middle. Let them see themselves in you.

3. Admiration: They want to be where you are. Share wins, transformations, and results. Show what's possible.

4. Unconditional Value: You give without expectation. Your free content is so good that people feel guilty not buying from you.

Share the knowledge. Sell the execution.

Give away everything you know for free. Charge for the implementation. When people go to act on what you've taught them, they realize execution is overwhelming. That's exactly where your offer comes in. The knowledge is free. The execution is what you sell.

Free Content → builds awareness + trust → Lead Magnet → captures email, delivers quick win → Low-Ticket ($10–$100) → removes barrier to entry → Mid-Ticket ($500–$5K) → committed buyers → High-Ticket ($10K+) → done-with-you or done-for-you

The CTA strategy

  • Awareness content: "Follow for more" or "Save this"
  • Trust-building content: "Download my free [lead magnet]"
  • Authority content: "Join the [community/program]"
  • Conversion content: "Apply to work with me"
